Why we watch volcanoes breathe

Every volcano leaks gas, even while it sleeps. Reading that breath — continuously, and from every volcano at once — is how NOVAC turns an invisible signal into an early warning.

Every volcano leaks gas

Even a "sleeping" volcano exhales sulfur dioxide, CO₂, and other gases every day. That quiet baseline is what scientists compare everything else against.

A sudden rise is a warning sign

When magma pushes closer to the surface, gas emissions often spike days or weeks before an eruption — giving nearby communities precious time to prepare.

Gas clouds are a hazard too

Volcanic SO₂ and ash can damage aircraft engines and harm air quality far beyond the crater rim — pilots and health agencies rely on this data daily.

One volcano can't tell the whole story

By comparing measurements from hundreds of volcanoes worldwide, researchers spot patterns no single observatory could see alone — that's exactly what NOVAC was built for.

Watching, day and night

Automated instruments now scan the sky around the clock, turning an invisible gas plume into a continuous, real-time signal of what's happening underground.

That's the whole point of NOVAC.

One global network, watching every volcano's breath — so the next warning sign is never missed.
